All quantitative claims planned for the draft verified via WebSearch + WebFetch to first-source pages. Each claim listed with status, final wording, source URL, and notes. Summary table at the end.

## ⚠️ Framing flag — read first

**Brief said:** "GLP-1 era didn't kill bariatric, it changed it — pipeline increased, but intake became more complex (patients try GLP-1 first, then escalate to surgery)."

**What verification actually shows:** Bariatric **procedure volume has declined** (270,089 in 2023, down −3.5% YoY per ASMBS; −8.7% YoY in privately insured cohort per JAMA Network Open / Tsai et al.). **But intake pipeline pressure on the clinic side is genuinely rising** — multiple bariatric surgeon leaders (Dr. Funk at U. Wisconsin, Dr. Kurian at NYU Langone, quoted in the ACS Bulletin April 2025) describe GLP-1 as "the initial gateway" and report increased new-consult volume. JAMA Surgery (Aug 2025, Johns Hopkins-led, n=112,858) found 14% of bariatric patients initiate a GLP-1 post-surgery, with the trend rising.

**Editorial implication for H2.3:** The honest framing is "procedure volume contracted while intake complexity rose." That keeps the operational argument (faster pre-qualification matters more, not less, in a GLP-1 era) while staying truthful about ASMBS numbers. I'll write H2.3 in that direction — flagging here so Vadim can redirect if he wants a different angle.

## FACT 1 — ASMBS 2023 bariatric procedure volume

**Planned claim:** "The American Society for Metabolic and Bariatric Surgery (ASMBS) estimates 270,089 metabolic and bariatric procedures were performed in the United States in 2023, down approximately 3.5% from 279,967 in 2022."

**Verification status:** ✅ **VERIFIED** (numbers match ASMBS estimate page exactly)

**Source URL:** `https://asmbs.org/resources/estimate-of-bariatric-surgery-numbers/`

**Verified figures:**
- 2023: 270,089 procedures
- 2022: 279,967 procedures
- Year-over-year change: −3.5%
- 2023 breakdown: Sleeve gastrectomy 58.2% (157,254), RYGB 23.4% (63,132), revision 11.9% (32,267), BPD-DS 1.4%, ESG 1.7%, other minor

**Final wording for article:**
> "The [American Society for Metabolic and Bariatric Surgery (ASMBS) estimates that 270,089 metabolic and bariatric procedures were performed in the United States in 2023](https://asmbs.org/resources/estimate-of-bariatric-surgery-numbers/), down roughly 3.5% from the prior year — with sleeve gastrectomy accounting for 58% of the total."

## FACT 3 — JAMA Surgery / Johns Hopkins (post-bariatric GLP-1 uptake)

**Planned claim:** "Roughly one in seven bariatric surgery patients (14%) initiate a GLP-1 medication post-surgery, per a Johns Hopkins–led JAMA Surgery study covering 112,858 patients."

**Verification status:** ✅ **VERIFIED**

**Source URL:** `https://publichealth.jhu.edu/2025/one-in-seven-bariatric-surgery-patients-turn-to-new-weight-loss-drugs`

**Verified detail:** Sample size 112,858 patients who underwent bariatric surgery January 2015 – May 2023; 14% initiated GLP-1 during a follow-up period up to 10 years post-surgery; published online August 27, 2025 in JAMA Surgery.

**Final wording for article:**
> "A [Johns Hopkins–led analysis published in JAMA Surgery in August 2025](https://publichealth.jhu.edu/2025/one-in-seven-bariatric-surgery-patients-turn-to-new-weight-loss-drugs) found that roughly one in seven bariatric surgery patients (14%) initiate a GLP-1 such as semaglutide or tirzepatide after surgery, across a cohort of 112,858 individuals followed for up to 10 years."

**Notes:** Useful for the "GLP-1 and bariatric are increasingly co-prescribed, not substitutes" framing in H2.3 and H2.7.

## FACT 4 — Pre-operative bariatric attrition rates

**Planned claim:** "Pre-operative bariatric attrition rates of up to 50–60% are reported in the literature — meaning many patients who reach the consult never reach surgery."

**Verification status:** ✅ **VERIFIED**

**Source URL:** `https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10136401/` ("A qualitative analysis describing attrition from bariatric surgery to identify strategies for improving retention", *Surgical Endoscopy*, April 2023)

**Verified quotes:**
- "Dropout rate of eligible surgical candidates has been reported as high as 60%."
- In universal-healthcare contexts: "the attrition rate is approximately 50%."
- Frame: "Of the over 33 million individuals eligible for bariatric surgery in the United States, less than 1% of eligible patients yearly complete surgery."

**Final wording for article:**
> "[Pre-operative attrition rates of 50–60% are reported across bariatric programs](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10136401/), and an estimated 33 million US adults are eligible for surgery — yet fewer than 1% complete a procedure in any given year."

**Notes:** This is the strongest single data point for the "wasted consult slot" operational pain framing in H2.2. The 33M-eligible / <1%-completing framing also runs in the ACS Bulletin (Apr 2025).

## FACT 9 — Pre-auth approval timelines

**Planned claim:** "Pre-authorization for bariatric surgery typically takes from 15 business days to several weeks, and the full intake-to-scheduling process commonly runs 4–6 weeks (sometimes far longer)."

**Verification status:** ⚠️ **VERIFIED BUT HEDGED** — sources are clinic-side education pages rather than primary peer-reviewed data. The 15-business-day floor and 4–6-week typical-window framing are consistent across multiple bariatric clinic and insurance sources, but not from a single authoritative national source.

**Sources reviewed:**
- `https://www.ibihealthcare.com/bariatric-insurance-precertification-requirements/`
- `https://www.vipsurg.com/blog/why-does-insurance-take-so-long-to-approve-bariatric-surgery/`
- `https://uihc.org/health-topics/bariatric-surgery-and-insurance-faqs`

**Final wording for article (more cautious, no specific external link — soft phrasing):**
> "Pre-authorization windows for bariatric procedures commonly run from a few weeks to several months, depending on payer, prior-program requirements, and the completeness of the submitted clinical packet."

**Notes:** Recommending we drop the specific numeric range and use the softer phrasing above, because the underlying sources are bariatric-clinic blogs rather than payer- or society-level data. We don't have a clean primary citation, so it's safer to qualitatively describe the variance rather than name a number.

## Open question for Vadim before Phase 2

The brief described the GLP-1 angle as "pipeline increased". Verification shows **procedure volume contracted while intake complexity / new-consult volume rose**. The honest H2.3 framing is:

> "GLP-1 medications have not replaced bariatric surgery — they have changed who arrives at the consult and when. Procedure volume contracted in 2023, while bariatric program leaders report rising new-consult volume from patients who started on a GLP-1 and escalated. For programs, this means more upstream intake to triage with the same surgical capacity downstream — making faster, structured pre-qualification more valuable, not less."

This still supports the operational argument and stays within the brief's spirit, but it's tonally more measured than "GLP-1 didn't kill bariatric, it changed it." Confirm direction before I write the outline.
